A way to rebuild your understanding of a system your agents built and you only directed.

The problem

When agents build a project across many sessions, the human ends up with a gap between what was built and what they actually understand about it. In normal development that understanding accumulates on its own. You are in the code, you see the review threads, you overhear the debugging. With agent-led work you get the outcome but miss the formation. You approved the plan, you see the result, and the middle is opaque.

That gap has a cost. You cannot answer good questions about architecture, tradeoffs, or risk. You cannot make the next-phase decision well. You cannot hand the work to another person or agent. And your own contribution, the direction and the judgment calls, gets lost, because the git history just shows an agent writing code.

What I built

A framework for reconstructing that mental model, designed against real projects rather than in the abstract. A few ideas do the work:

  • Triggered by need, not by milestone. A debrief fires on a forcing function: someone asks what you built, the work changes hands, you come back after a gap, a decision is due. The trigger is what you have to do next, not the state of the project.
  • Three sources, three biases. Knowledge in an agent-built project lives in three places, and each one lies in its own way. The human remembers the project they meant to build. The code reads as more deliberate than it was. The session and decision logs, written by agents with no memory between sessions, optimize for legibility over fidelity.
  • Triangulation over reconciliation. The debrief reads all three against each other and treats their disagreements as the point. Where the record and the memory diverge becomes content, not something to quietly smooth over.
  • A living substrate, not a one-off document. Each forcing function runs a reconstruction pass that updates one durable artifact. Sections carry their own history, and specific views (a stakeholder one-pager, an onboarding pack, a friction map) get exported from it on demand.

What it made possible

You walk out of a debrief with a meaningfully better grip on your own system than you walked in with, and an artifact that carries it forward. You can field the architecture questions, bring in another contributor, and decide the next phase without re-reading the codebase end to end. It also gives you back the part the git log erases: the direction you set and the calls you made.

Why it matters

This is the part of agent-built work that almost nobody is designing for yet. We are getting very good at shipping software through agents and not yet good at staying in command of what we shipped. claude-debrief is a structured answer to that, built and stress-tested on a project with roughly 125 sessions behind it and a thin-tier project as the counterweight, so the framework has to hold at both ends.
