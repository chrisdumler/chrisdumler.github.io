CoLa

The consent, boundary, and negotiation layer for cross-user agent interaction.

The problem

Agents are starting to talk to other agents. Booking, negotiating, fetching, and acting on behalf of the people who run them. The transport protocols for that conversation are arriving fast: Google's A2A, ANP, NLIP. They define how agents can talk.

What they don't define is what an agent is allowed to do. When my agent meets yours, who consented to what? Where are the boundaries? How do two agents negotiate a request that neither user explicitly anticipated? Transport without governance is just a faster way to do something nobody agreed to.

The approach

CoLa is the layer that sits above transport and answers those questions. It treats consent not as a checkbox but as a living, negotiable contract between agents, legible to both the humans behind them and the systems enforcing it.

  • Consent: explicit, scoped grants for what an agent may do on a user's behalf, revocable and inspectable.
  • Boundaries: the hard edges a request can't cross, independent of how persuasive the other agent is.
  • Negotiation: a structured way for two agents to find an outcome inside both users' boundaries, or fail safely when there isn't one.

Why it matters

This is the seam I find most interesting: the gap between a system that asks and one that assumes. Get the governance layer right and agent-to-agent interaction can be genuinely useful without being quietly coercive. Get it wrong and we've automated the erosion of consent at protocol speed.

CoLa is a bet that the trustworthy version is designable, and a working argument for what it should look like.
