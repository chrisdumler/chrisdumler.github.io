The problem

Audio and voice projects keep hitting the same questions. Who actually owns this model? What does the license permit on the audio it generates, not just on the weights? Is the project still maintained, or did the last release ship a year ago? What is the free tier in practice versus what the marketing page implies? Which other models quietly share the same engine?

Answer those questions inside each project and the knowledge fragments. It decays as models change, and it gets rediscovered mid-integration, which is the worst time to find out a license forbids the thing you just built.

What I built

AMATLAS centralizes that diligence into one structured entry per model. Each entry pairs a YAML contract with a narrative body, so it is both machine-readable and honest about the parts that resist a checkbox.

  • Structured fields: identity and parent company, engine lineage, architecture family, access methods with per-method licensing, voice surface, watermarking and provenance, languages, and a maintenance signal that says whether the model is still alive.
  • A lineage graph: edges between entries (derives-from, sibling-of, successor-to) so you can see which models are really the same family wearing different names.
  • An entry lifecycle: every entry moves through draft, verified, and consumer-vetted, with an agentic verifier that re-checks the facts instead of trusting them to stay true.
  • A decision log: the catalog documents its own rules, so the schema can evolve without quietly breaking the projects that depend on it.

What it made possible

Downstream projects cite atlas entries instead of restating the research. The diligence lives in one place, stays current, and gets pulled in rather than redone. Its first consumer is a synthetic audio research project that needed a clear, trustworthy map of which models could actually be used and how.

Why it matters

This is knowledge treated as infrastructure. The audio model space moves fast and rewards whoever can keep the facts straight, and AMATLAS is a bet that the way to do that is to build the reference, verify it, and let everything else read from it. It is the kind of work I reach for when a domain is messy and the cost of getting it wrong shows up later, somewhere expensive.
