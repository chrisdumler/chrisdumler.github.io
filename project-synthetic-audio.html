Synthetic audio research

Generating convincing speech across the whole fast-moving frontier of voice models.

The challenge

Text-to-speech moves faster than almost any other corner of AI. New voice models land constantly, and each one arrives with its own character, its own strengths and failure modes, and its own awkward setup. Working seriously with synthetic voice means more than picking one model and calling an API. It means being able to generate across the whole moving frontier, and keeping up as it shifts month to month.

What I built

A synthetic-voice generation pipeline and the research practice around it, spanning more than twenty models and well over a hundred thousand generated samples. At a high level it does a few things well:

  • Generation across many models. Voice samples are produced from a wide set of text-to-speech systems, from open-weights models you self-host to commercial APIs.
  • Deliberate model selection. Which models go in is a researched decision, and each candidate gets real diligence before it earns a place. That recurring need is what grew into AMATLAS, a reusable knowledge base of audio models.
  • Reproducible by design. Each model runs in its own reproducible environment, so a generation run from months ago can be rebuilt and rerun exactly, and the work scales out across GPU cloud rather than living on one machine.
  • Evaluation as a first-class step. Generated audio is scored and, where automated measures run out of road, judged by human listening panels.

Where the AI comes in

This is also an example of agent-operated research. The program runs through Claude across multiple months, with the model facilitating the hands-on work: vetting and integrating new models into the pipeline, running and scoring experiments, and keeping the decision and session record that lets a months-long investigation stay coherent. It is the same way of working as the rest of my projects, applied to a serious, long-running research effort.

Why it matters

Synthetic voice is becoming a normal material to build with, and the gap is widening between people who can use one model and people who can work fluently across the whole field. This project is where I built that fluency: a disciplined, repeatable way to generate and study voice across many models at once, and a clear picture of how they actually behave once you push them.
