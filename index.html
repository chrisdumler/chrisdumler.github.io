Personalized AI Collaboration: From Generic Assistants to Real Teammates

What it is: Most AI assistants are designed for "everyone," which means they work well for no one. I created personalized AI collaborators designed around specific thinking patterns - like Pemtu, an AI PM built for ADHD brains and startup pace.

How it works: Instead of generic prompts, I design AI characters with explicit role definitions, communication protocols, and understanding of individual thinking styles. Pemtu knows how to brainstorm with rapid ideation, manage AI coding agents, and maintain systematic documentation - all while matching my natural workflow patterns.

Why it matters: This turns AI from a tool you manage into a teammate you collaborate with. The multi-agent research system was just proof of concept - built in a 2.5-hour sprint because the collaboration felt natural instead of like managing a weird tool. There are ethical questions about AI that adapts to individual patterns, but they're worth exploring.

Amara Knowledge System: Solving the AI Memory Problem

What it is: AI conversations are stateless, which creates constant recapping overhead and token waste. I invented a "bookmark" DSL - a simple, parseable format that captures just enough context for an AI to resume conversations naturally without database complexity.

How it works: Instead of long conversation summaries, Amara maintains a single-line bookmark with timestamp, context, references, current state, and next actions. She updates it automatically based on conversation cues. For deeper context, she can reference specific files in a sandboxed filesystem of 100s of markdown documents.

Why it matters: This system has evolved through constant use and iteration. I built AKS2 with more sophisticated features, but returned to AKS1 because dependability beats cleverness when you're using something daily. The bookmark DSL is simple enough to be reliable, but flexible enough to handle complex project continuity. After months of daily collaboration, it feels like working with someone who actually remembers our shared context.

Understanding AI Communication Patterns

What it is

Systematic methodology for understanding how AI models process context and make communication decisions. Through competitive analysis (HackAPrompt 2.0), I developed frameworks for analyzing AI behavior patterns that reveal how models interpret authority, constraints, and reasoning environments. Achieved top-100 global ranking using systematic behavioral analysis rather than just throwing prompts at the wall and seeing what sticks.

Core techniques discovered

Three key patterns for understanding AI communication:

Reasoning Process Integration
Not all models can distinguish between their own thinking and properly formatted reasoning injected into prompts. This reveals how they process internal vs external context.
Contextual Authority Engineering
Models construct beliefs about permissions and constraints based on contextual signals rather than having fixed authority structures.
Constraint Reframing
Apparent limitations can often be worked within through appropriate contextual framing, revealing the difference between hard and soft boundaries.
1. Probe What does this model reveal? ‚Üí 2. Analyze How does it process context? ‚Üí 3. Design Create better interactions

Systematic approach: understand each model's communication patterns, then design interactions that work naturally within those patterns while achieving desired outcomes.

Why this matters for interaction design

Understanding how models actually interpret context is essential for building better AI systems. This methodology reveals the patterns behind AI decision-making, enabling designers to create interactions that guide models toward better responses while respecting necessary boundaries. It's the difference between crossing your fingers and actually knowing what you're doing.

Practical realities

No technique works on every model. Different systems process context differently - some exposed reasoning that could be analyzed, others didn't. What the systematic approach provided was structured ways to quickly test hypotheses and adapt when approaches didn't work, rather than random trial and error. The competition's token efficiency constraints also taught valuable lessons about identifying minimal viable context needed for effective interaction design.

Applications for AI interaction design

  • Evaluation systems: Systematic frameworks for testing AI behavior across different interaction patterns
  • User experience optimization: Understanding how to design prompts and interfaces that naturally elicit better responses
  • Safety and robustness: Identifying failure modes and designing more resilient behavioral boundaries
  • Training insights: Data for improving how models learn to interact effectively
