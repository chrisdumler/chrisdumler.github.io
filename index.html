A lot of creative work starts before the idea is clean: a phrase, a sound, a mood, a weird little signal that feels like it has something in it.

That's where I work. I push the model off its defaults: synthetic voices, strange inputs, broken formatting, character constraints, red-team-style experiments. Those early signals become material the artist can react to. The judgment stays human.

The point isn't to make AI seem magical. It's to make it usable as a creative material.

My approach

  • Start with the actual creative problem

    Don't force the person into the tool. Figure out what they're trying to explore, make, hear, test, or understand.

  • Build small working things

    I'd rather make a rough prototype than talk about a perfect concept. The prototype tells you what is real.

  • Experiment at the edge of the model

    Some of the best results come from using AI slightly wrong: strange prompts, synthetic voices, broken inputs, character constraints, and noticing what breaks.

About me

I'm Chris. I scaled the data team at Tinder through its startup-to-IPO years, taught mindfulness, built software, made music with AI, designed characters, and built synthetic audio workflows.

I'm good at translating vague creative intent into working systems. I like teaching through making, and I'm most useful when the brief is still fuzzy and we need to build something to understand it.

I'm useful in creative rooms where the question is still forming. I can sit with curiosity, make tools quickly, explain what's happening, and follow the weird parts without losing the thread.
